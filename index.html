The Sahel

A region at the crossroads of climate, conflict, and culture.

Introduction to the Sahel

The Sahel is a broad band of land where the Sahara meets the savannah, stretching from Senegal in the west to Sudan in the east. It is home to traders, herders, farmers, and cities that sit on old caravan routes.

Today, the region faces overlapping pressures: climate stress, political instability, armed groups, and humanitarian crises. Understanding the Sahel means understanding how all of these forces interact across borders.

Militant Groups in the Sahel

Several armed groups operate across Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger, and the wider Sahel. They differ in their ideology, organization, and areas of operation.

  • JNIM – Al-Qaeda–aligned umbrella group active across Mali, Burkina Faso, and parts of Niger.
  • ISSP – Islamic State Sahel Province, operating mainly in the tri-border area around eastern Mali and western Niger.
  • Ansaru & others – Smaller factions with roots in Nigeria and local networks.

Each profile explains origins, leadership, ideology, and main regions of activity.

Countries in Focus

The Sahel spans multiple states, and each country experiences the crisis in a different way. Some are at the center of current violence; others are more exposed to spillover and cross-border movements.

  • Mali – Epicenter of jihadist insurgency and repeated political crises.
  • Burkina Faso – Rapidly expanding militant presence and rural displacement.
  • Niger – Tri-border frontline and key transit hub.
  • Chad – Regional military actor connected to wider Sahel dynamics.
  • Mauritania – Important political and security partner in the region.

Key Regions & Frontlines

Conflict is concentrated in specific corridors and borderlands rather than evenly spread across the map. These zones often cross state boundaries.

  • Liptako–Gourma – The tri-border area where JNIM and ISSP are most active.
  • Central Mali – Mopti and Ségou regions, shaped by jihadist influence and communal violence.
  • Northern Burkina Faso – Rural zones with shrinking state presence.
  • Western Niger – Tillabéri region, targeted by ISSP attacks.
  • Coastal Spillover – Northern Benin, Togo, and Côte d’Ivoire.
