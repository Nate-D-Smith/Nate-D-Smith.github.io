Terrorism In The Sahel

Introduction to the Sahel

The Sahel is a region in Africa where the Sahara Desert and the Sudanian Savanna meet. The climate is very hot and dry and food is scarce. The people who inhabit the Sahel have always had difficulties with finding food and water due to this climate. Because of this many of the people who inhabit the area are nomadic pastoralists such as the Tuareg and Fulani ethnic groups. These groups have very often been oppressed and discriminated against by the southern peoples of the Sahel. In recent history, many of These people have turned to terrorism as a means of overcoming their oppression.

In the West, when we think of terorism, we usually think of groups like ISIS and al-Qaeda and countries like Syria, Iraq, and Afghanistan. While it is true that these countries and groups were once the global hotspot for terrorism, we have not shifted our perception of the issue even as the location of the issue has shifted. In the past 5 years, it has become clear that the Sahel is the new epicenter of global terrorism.

The Sahel: The New Epicenter of Global Terrorism

In 2024, militant groups in the Sahel were responsible for more terrorism related deaths than the rest of the world combined! These 3,885 victims were killed by groups such as Jama'at Nasr al-Islam wal-Muslimin (JNIM), Islamic State Sahel Province (ISSP), Boko Haram, Ansaru, and Islamic State West Africa Province.

Each group has different motives, ideologies, and methods, and each country has its own culture, struggles, and conter-terrorism strategies. Though there are dozens of deadly groups in the Sahel each with their own impact on the regional security crisis, we will focus on JNIM, ISSP, and Boko Haram.

In 2024, 5 of the 10 countries most impacted by terrorism globally were in the Sahel. These are Burkina Faso (1), Mali (4), Niger (5), Nigeria (6), and Cameroon (10). Though all 5 of thsese countries have suffered significantly from terrorism, we will focus on Burkina Faso, Mali, and Niger. These 3 countries share many similarities including the existential nature of the terror threat, their shared French colonial heritage, and the relationship between their military junta governments.
