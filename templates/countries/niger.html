Niger

Introduction

Niger is a vast, landlocked country in the central Sahel, defined by desert expanses, trans-Saharan trade routes, and a population concentrated along the Niger River and southern agricultural zones. Historically, Niger served as a crossroads for commerce, migration, and Islamic scholarship, while colonial rule under France reshaped political authority and borders. Since independence in 1960, Niger has faced recurring governance challenges, military interventions in politics, and persistent development constraints.

Over the past decade, Niger has become a critical front in the Sahel’s security crisis. Pressured simultaneously from the west by jihadist groups operating in Mali and Burkina Faso and from the southeast by Boko Haram–linked factions around Lake Chad, Niger occupies a uniquely exposed position. Despite maintaining relatively cohesive state institutions, the country has struggled to contain the spread of armed violence across its vast and lightly governed territories.

Militant Groups and Violence

Jihadist violence in Niger is primarily driven by two distinct theaters. In the western regions, particularly Tillabéri and Tahoua, Islamic State Sahel Province (ISSP) and al-Qaeda–aligned elements linked to JNIM conduct attacks against military forces, local officials, and civilian communities. These groups exploit porous borders and rural grievances, frequently launching cross-border operations that blur the line between national conflicts in Mali, Burkina Faso, and Niger.

In the southeast, the Diffa region has been heavily affected by violence associated with Boko Haram and its splinter factions operating around Lake Chad. Attacks in this zone often target villages, security posts, and economic infrastructure, producing chronic displacement and humanitarian strain. Although Niger has avoided complete territorial collapse, the combination of multi-front jihadist pressure, limited resources, and political instability has made it one of the most strategically significant—and vulnerable—states in the Sahel.
