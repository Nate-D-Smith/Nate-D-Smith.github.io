Ménaka, Mali

VEI: 91

Introduction

Ménaka is a sparsely populated region in eastern Mali bordering Niger. Formerly part of the Gao Region, Ménaka became its own administrative region in 2016. The area is largely desert, with communities that rely on pastoralism, trade, and cross-border movement for livelihoods.

Limited state presence, vast distances, and porous borders have historically made the region difficult to govern, contributing to long-standing insecurity and weak service provision.

Jihadism in Ménaka

Since 2016, Ménaka has emerged as one of the most severely affected regions in Mali’s jihadist conflict. The area has been a core zone of operations for Islamic State in the Greater Sahara (ISGS), which has targeted civilians, local militias, and state forces.

Violence in Ménaka has been characterized by mass-casualty attacks, forced displacement, and sustained pressure on local communities. The region frequently experiences some of the highest fatality counts per attack in Mali, making it particularly dangerous for civilians and humanitarian access.

Violence Exposure Index

Ménaka has a Violence Exposure Index (VEI) of 20 out of 100, indicating moderate relative exposure to jihadist violence. VEI is calculated using total terrorist attacks and deaths recorded in the Global Terrorism Database (GTD) since 2000, with incidents occurring after 2015 given greater weight [1].

It is important to note that attacks occurring in what is now Ménaka were recorded under Gao in the GTD prior to 2016, as Ménaka did not yet exist as a separate administrative region. As a result, historical violence trends reflect a broader geographic area before the regional split.
