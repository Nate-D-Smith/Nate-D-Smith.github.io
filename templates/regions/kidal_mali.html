Kidal, Mali

VEI: 94

Introduction

Kidal is a remote region in northeastern Mali bordering Algeria and Niger. Predominantly desert and sparsely populated, the region has long been characterized by limited state presence, cross-border movement, and historical tensions between local communities and the central government. These structural conditions have shaped Kidal’s security environment for decades.

Jihadism in Kidal

Kidal has been one of the most persistently unstable regions in Mali since the outbreak of rebellion and jihadist insurgency in 2012. Armed groups affiliated with al-Qaeda and the Islamic State, as well as local armed factions, have operated extensively in the region, targeting security forces, peacekeepers, and rival groups.

The region’s proximity to Algeria and its role as a transit corridor for weapons, fighters, and illicit goods have made it a strategic stronghold for militant organizations. Periods of reduced violence are often followed by renewed confrontation, reflecting the fragility of security arrangements in the area.

Violence Exposure Index

Kidal has a Violence Exposure Index (VEI) of 94 out of 100, indicating extremely high relative exposure to jihadist violence. VEI is calculated using the frequency and lethality of terrorist attacks recorded in the Global Terrorism Database (GTD) since 2000, with incidents after 2015 receiving greater weight [1].

Due to its long-standing role as a center of armed activity, Kidal consistently ranks among the most violence-affected regions in Mali, making humanitarian access and sustained operations particularly challenging.
