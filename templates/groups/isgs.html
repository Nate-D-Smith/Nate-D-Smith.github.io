Islamic State in the Greater Sahara (ISGS)

Introduction and foundation

The Islamic State in the Greater Sahara (ISGS) is a Salafi-jihadist militant organization operating primarily in the tri-border region of Mali, Niger, and Burkina Faso. The group emerged in 2015 as a splinter faction from al-Mourabitoun under the leadership of Adnan Abu Walid al-Sahrawi and formally pledged allegiance to the Islamic State in 2016. ISGS was subsequently recognized by the Islamic State’s central leadership as part of its global network.

ISGS draws much of its manpower from Fulani and other Sahelian communities affected by insecurity, marginalization, and state absence. The group initially operated as a relatively small mobile force but rapidly expanded its operational tempo after 2018, becoming one of the deadliest actors in the Sahel. Following the death of al-Sahrawi in 2021, ISGS has continued to function through decentralized command structures aligned with Islamic State’s broader West African strategy.

Ideology and methods

ISGS adheres to the Islamic State’s uncompromising interpretation of Salafi-jihadism, emphasizing strict takfir (excommunication), territorial conquest, and the use of extreme violence as a tool of governance. The group rejects compromise with local authorities and views populations that do not submit to its rule as legitimate targets. Loyalty to the Islamic State’s caliphate project remains central to ISGS’s identity and operational logic.

Unlike JNIM’s more pragmatic “hearts and minds” approach, ISGS relies heavily on mass-casualty attacks, village massacres, and collective punishment to assert control. Civilians, militias, and rival jihadist groups are frequently targeted to eliminate resistance and intimidate surrounding communities. ISGS has focused much of its violence on rural areas, exploiting weak state presence to establish zones of coercive dominance.

Regional Impact and Current Trajectory

ISGS has played a central role in transforming the Sahel into one of the world’s most lethal theaters of jihadist violence. Its expansion across western Niger, northern Burkina Faso, and eastern Mali has resulted in large-scale civilian displacement, village destruction, and persistent insecurity. The group’s rivalry with JNIM has further intensified violence, with clashes between the two jihadist camps adding an additional layer of instability to already fragile regions.

Strategically, ISGS prioritizes territorial control and population subjugation over political negotiation or gradual infiltration. While this approach has generated rapid gains in some areas, it has also provoked sustained military pressure and local resistance. Despite leadership losses and counterterrorism operations, ISGS remains a highly adaptive and dangerous actor whose continued presence shapes the security dynamics of the central Sahel.
